    Shortcut: glance at the bottom panel (residual) first. If it looks nearly flat, the codec kept almost everything.

    What is a waveform? The simplest picture of sound — it shows loudness over time. Tall peaks = loud; flat line = silence. This is the most direct way to see whether the AI preserved the overall shape and dynamics of your audio.

    Three panels stacked vertically: The top is the original, the middle is what the AI rebuilt, and the bottom is the difference (what was lost). If the top two look almost identical, quality is high. If the bottom panel has prominent peaks, significant audio was discarded.

    Chart 2 of 4 — Spectrogram Comparison
    Click to enlarge
    Click to expand full-size

    Shortcut: compare the top edges of both panels. If the right looks fuzzier or darker at the top, high-pitched sounds were softened.

    What is a spectrogram? A spectrogram shows which frequencies are present at each moment — like an X-ray for sound. Horizontal axis = time; vertical axis = pitch (low sounds at bottom, high sounds at top); colour = volume (bright yellow/white = loud, dark = quiet).

    Left panel = original, right panel = reconstructed. Similar colour patterns mean frequencies were preserved. Watch the top portion (high frequencies — consonants in speech, shimmer in music). Blurring or darkening at the top of the right panel means high frequencies were partially lost.

    Click to enlarge

    Process transparency: this chart shows the AI's internal representation — not the audio output, but the decisions the model made to produce it. Row 1 (top) carries the most critical structure; each row below adds progressively finer detail.

    A window inside the AI's decision-making. Instead of storing raw audio samples, the neural codec translates your sound into a sequence of short numerical codes — like choosing entries from a learned dictionary. This chart shows which codes the AI selected, moment by moment, at each level of detail.

    Each horizontal row = one codebook layer. Row 1 = coarse structure (most perceptually important). Row 8 = finest residual detail (least critical, discarded first under bandwidth pressure). Each column = a 13 ms slice of time.

    Varied, shifting colours = complex audio requiring diverse codes. Repeating patterns = simpler, more predictable content. Abrupt colour changes in Row 1 = the model detected a significant acoustic event (onset, transient, phoneme boundary).

    Chart 4 of 4 — AI Vocabulary Efficiency
    Click to enlarge
    Click to expand full-size

    What it means: high values across all three charts = your audio was complex and the AI worked hard to represent it accurately. Low values = simpler content, fewer distinct codes needed.

    Three bar charts measure how efficiently the AI used its code vocabulary. Each bar = one codebook layer (layer 1 = coarse, layer 8 = fine detail).

    • Entropy (left) — how unpredictable the codes are. High entropy = the AI used many different codes (complex audio). Values above 8 bits indicate very rich, varied content.
    • Usage Rate (centre) — what % of 1,024 available codes were actually used. Higher = more variety needed to represent the sound.
    • Temporal Change Rate (right) — how quickly codes change frame-to-frame. High values (>90%) = rapidly changing audio (speech, busy music).
    Codec
    Short for "coder-decoder". A codec is software that compresses audio (making files smaller) and then decompresses it for playback.
    Neural codec
    A codec powered by a neural network (AI). It learns patterns in audio to achieve much higher compression than traditional codecs.
    Compression
    Reducing file size by removing or approximating information. More compression = smaller file, but potentially lower quality.
    Latency
    The delay introduced by processing. "50 ms latency" means the system takes 50 milliseconds to process each chunk of audio. Lower is faster.
    Residual audio
    The difference between the original and the reconstructed audio — i.e., the sound that was "lost" during compression.
    dB (decibels)
    A unit for measuring signal levels. Higher dB values in SNR/SI-SDR mean better quality (less distortion).
    MCD (Mel-Cepstral Distortion)
    A perceptually motivated quality metric. It measures how much the compressed audio differs from the original in the mel-frequency domain — the same frequency scale used by human hearing. Lower is better: below 3 dB is excellent, 3–5 dB is good.
    MOS (Mean Opinion Score)
    A 1–5 quality rating scale widely used in audio and speech research: 5 = excellent, 4 = good, 3 = fair, 2 = poor, 1 = bad. The "MOS estimate" here is derived from MCD and approximates how a listener would rate the audio.
    kbps (kilobits per second)
    A measure of data rate. A music CD uses ~1,411 kbps. A neural codec can achieve similar perceived quality at 6–24 kbps.
    Spectrogram
    A visual display of audio showing which frequencies are present over time. Bright colours = loud; dark = quiet. High on the chart = high-pitched sound.
